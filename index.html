Lunar South Pole / PSR Environmental Characterization Mission
1.6   System Evaluation Criteria

System selection shall be driven by the ability of each candidate architecture to close the mission at the CDH level: preserve command authority, protect science data integrity, and sustain autonomous fault recovery across the lunar south pole PSR environment. Because orbital geometry, surface illumination, thermal extremes, radiation exposure, and contact opportunities all constrain the mission simultaneously, the evaluation must treat signal processing, power efficiency, and data throughput as coupled system trades rather than independent subsystem metrics. The preferred option is the one that satisfies the verified mission requirements with the smallest resource penalty and the lowest demonstrable implementation risk. Any value not anchored to an approved requirement, interface control document, or verified analysis shall remain `[TBR]`.

Primary Evaluation Criteria
Criterion Decision Focus Required Evidence
Mission closure Does the CDH architecture preserve command, telemetry, and safe-mode control under PSR thermal, radiation, and communications constraints? Requirement trace, hazard closure, fault response logic.
Resource efficiency Does the option fit the allocated mass, volume, power, storage, and lifecycle cost envelopes without consuming critical mission margin? Mass properties, power budget, storage budget, cost basis.
Accommodation Can the subsystem be placed, routed, and thermally managed without violating keep-out zones, antenna field-of-regard, or structural loads? Packaging study, thermal model, interface control review.
Mission risk Is the architecture reliable, manufacturable, and mature enough to verify within the mission schedule? TRL basis, heritage review, verification plan, BOM availability.
Resource & Cost Constraints
Measure Evaluation Rule CDH Implication
Mass and volume Prefer the smallest verified implementation that preserves harness routing, service access, and margin to the approved spacecraft envelope. Do not trade away survival margin for packaging convenience.
Power and energy Score by average draw, peak draw, startup transients, and survival-mode load across cruise, descent, checkout, and PSR operations. Lower energy per delivered bit is favored when science return is equal.
Data throughput Evaluate acquisition rate, buffering, packetization, compression, error detection, and downlink closure against verified contact windows. The best option protects science fidelity before it maximizes raw bitrate.
Lifecycle cost Include procurement, integration, test, operations, rework, and spares; values not yet sourced remain `[TBR]`. Lowest total cost only matters after mission closure is satisfied.
Accommodation & Environment
Measure Evaluation Rule CDH Implication
Placement and interfaces Check board placement, connector orientation, cable bend radius, access for test, and compatibility with the spacecraft layout. Poor placement is a program risk, not a minor integration issue.
Field of regard Confirm antenna patterns, sensor viewing geometry, and keep-out volumes are clear across expected attitude states and terrain masking. Communications availability is a mission constraint, not an afterthought.
Thermal, radiation, and dust Assess dissipation paths, shielding, part tolerance, contamination sensitivity, and performance drift in the PSR environment. The preferred architecture minimizes degradation, not just initial performance.
Structural integration Verify launch, descent, landing, and rover-induced loads do not break alignment, solder joints, or retention features. Mechanical simplicity is preferred when it preserves reliability.
Mission Risk & Maturity
Measure Evaluation Rule CDH Implication
Reliability and fault containment Prefer architectures with explicit watchdogs, safe modes, EDAC, and single-fault tolerance on critical command and data paths. A recoverable fault is acceptable; a silent data loss mode is not.
System complexity Reject unnecessary software branching, coupled state machines, and custom interfaces that expand verification burden. Simpler command logic is easier to validate and operate.
TRL and heritage Favor flight-proven or environment-proven hardware where mission performance is comparable; critical-path items should be justified by verified heritage. Low-TRL options require a formal risk case, not optimism.
Manufacturability Check supplier availability, assembly access, reworkability, test coverage, and verification flow against the team schedule. An elegant design that cannot be built is not a viable selection.
Selection rule
If two candidates satisfy the same mission requirements, choose the option with lower verified power per delivered bit, stronger fault containment, higher TRL, and simpler integration. If a parameter is not yet fixed by the mission baseline, carry it as `[TBR]` rather than inventing a value.
